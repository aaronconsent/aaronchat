# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Owner-operators of home-service shops around Lake Livingston, Texas — HVAC and plumbing first, then electricians, roofers, fencing, septic, tree service and the rest of the trades the grading engine covers. One to fifteen trucks. The owner is usually the one who'd buy this, and he is reading the site on a phone between jobs, in a truck, with dirty hands and four minutes. He is not a marketer, has been sold to badly before, and can smell an agency pitch instantly.

The job he's trying to get done is not "buy marketing." It is "figure out why the phone isn't ringing as much as it used to, and whether the guy across town is beating me." Success is him running his free report card, seeing a grade he doesn't like, and believing there's a specific, affordable fix.

## Product Purpose

Top of Class Marketing is a one-man, AI-run marketing department for home-service contractors. The site's job is to convert a cold local contractor into a graded lead: get him to look up his own shop, confront his grade and his rank against local rivals, and hand over an email for the full report card and action plan. Everything downstream — the plans, the outreach, the build — depends on that one conversion.

Success is a completed report-card lookup with contact details attached, and secondarily a booked call.

## Positioning

The only marketing company that publicly grades its clients — and its prospects — every week, and tells you to fire it if the grade doesn't move.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: "Get your free report card." Secondary fallback: call 713-384-8985 for anyone who'd rather talk than type.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: your grade goes up, or you fire us.
- Belief ladder, in order: (1) this grade is real and it is about *my* business, not a generic audit; (2) I am behind shops I actually compete with, by name; (3) the gap is specific and fixable, and I can see exactly what's broken; (4) this guy can actually fix it — he has shipped real sites and run real marketing organizations; (5) $300 a month is a rounding error against what the gap is costing me; (6) there is no trap — month to month, publicly scored.
- Proof on hand: the grading engine itself (2,149 graded shops, refreshed weekly, every claim traceable to public Google data); the twelve live client projects on `/work/` shown as built work; Aaron's career record on `/about/` (Chief Business Officer at cPanel, CMO at Monarx, 20+ years). **There are no client testimonials, no named client outcomes, and no performance metrics.** The agency is pre-first-client for this brand.

## Brand Personality

Blunt, confident, anti-agency. Plain contractor English — short sentences, real dollar figures, no adjectives doing work that numbers should do. It talks the way a good tradesman talks about another trade's work: direct, unimpressed by polish, willing to tell you your setup is bad. Confidence comes from showing the scoreboard, never from adjectives about itself. The register is challenger, not chummy; it never begs, never hypes, and never says "excited" or "thrilled."

The emotional arc the site is built to produce: a jolt of *I did not realize I was losing to that guy*, immediately followed by *and this is fixable for less than I spend on fuel.*

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS / AI-startup.** Purple-to-blue gradients, floating glass cards, abstract 3D blobs, stock photos of teams collaborating. The default AI aesthetic; it would make the grading data look invented.
- **Big-city agency polish.** The Dallas/Houston firm look — full-bleed video hero, awards row, impersonal gloss. This is the thing being positioned against; looking like it defeats the argument.
- **Cheap contractor template.** The GoDaddy/Wix look the prospects already have — clip-art tools, badge soup, stretched logos. The site must visibly out-class the sites it's grading.
- **Literal schoolhouse clip-art.** Cartoon apples, crayon fonts, cutesy chalkboards. The report-card metaphor must stay sharp and adult; the moment it goes childish, the grade stops feeling like a real judgment.

## Design Principles

**Show the receipts, never claim them.** Every number on the site traces to real, public, verifiable data. No invented testimonials, no fabricated metrics, no "300% increase" claims. Where something is modeled or projected rather than measured, it is labeled as an estimate in the interface itself. This is a hard constraint, not a preference — the entire proposition is that the grade is honest, and one invented number poisons all of it.

**The grade is the product.** The report card is not a lead-magnet gimmick bolted to an agency site; it is the mechanic the whole business runs on. Design decisions serve legibility of the grade — the letter, the rank, the six factors, the gap.

**Practice what you preach.** The site is the demo. A visitor deciding whether a $300/mo AI-built site is any good is looking at one. Every interaction has to survive that inspection.

**Name the rival.** Abstract urgency does nothing; a specific competitor with a specific better grade does everything. Prefer the concrete local fact over the general claim, always.

**Earn the click before the pitch.** Free, specific value first — the grade, the rank, the fix list. The plan pricing comes after the visitor already believes the diagnosis.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA. Body text at 4.5:1 minimum against its background and large text at 3:1 — non-negotiable, and the audience skews 40+ reading outdoors on a phone in daylight, so treat AA as the floor rather than the goal. Visible keyboard focus on every interactive element. Every animation needs a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative. Never encode meaning in color alone: the grade badges must carry the letter, not just the green/gold/red.
